## Concept Inventory

### 1. Reinforcement learning setting

RL is a setting, not a single algorithm.

You have:

- an **agent**

- an **environment**

- **actions** taken by the agent

- **observations/states** and **rewards** returned by the environment

The objective is to learn behaviour that maximises long-run reward.

In this lecture, the environment is still modell as an **MDP**.

---

### 2. The flaky tram MDP

The running example remains the same:

- states are locations $1,2,\dots,6$

- from location $i$:

    - **walk** goes to $i+1$

    - **tram** tries to go to $2i$

- tram can fail with probability $0.1$

- rewards are negative costs:

    - walking tends to give $-1$

    - tram tends to give $-2$

- the goal is to reach the terminal state with highest total utility

This example is useful because it is simple enough to compute exactly, but rich enough to illustrate RL ideas.

---

### 3. Utility / return

A rollout (trajectory, episode) produces a discounted sum of rewards:

$$  
\text{utility}(\tau) = r_1 + \gamma r_2 + \gamma^2 r_3 + \cdots  
$$

If $\gamma = 1$, this is just the ordinary sum of rewards.

This lecture uses “utility” as essentially the same idea as “expected return.”

---

### 4. Value functions

The lecture reviews four important value notions:

#### Policy value

$$  
V_\pi(s)  
$$

Expected utility starting from state $s$ and following policy $\pi$.

#### Policy action-value

$$  
Q_\pi(s,a)  
$$

Expected utility if you first take action $a$ in state $s$, then follow $\pi$ thereafter.

#### Optimal state value

$$  
V^*(s)  
$$

Expected utility from state $s$ under the optimal policy.

#### Optimal action-value

$$  
Q^*(s,a)  
$$

Expected utility if you first take action $a$ in state $s$, then behave optimally thereafter.

A crucial idea:

- $V$ values live on states

- $Q$ values live on state–action pairs

---

### 5. Policy vs RL algorithm

This distinction matters.

A **policy** is a fixed mapping from states to actions.

An **RL algorithm** is a learning process that updates its internal estimates over time. It may begin with a poor policy and gradually improve it.

So the algorithm is dynamic; the policy it currently induces may change after every interaction.

---

### 6. Three major RL families

The lecture ends up organizing RL into three families.

#### Model-based RL

Learn the MDP first:

- estimate transitions

- estimate rewards

- solve the estimated MDP, often with value iteration

Then extract the policy.

#### Value-based RL

Do not estimate the MDP directly.

Instead, estimate values such as $Q(s,a)$, then derive the policy via something like:

$$  
\pi(s) = \arg\max_a Q(s,a)  
$$

#### Policy-based RL

Do not estimate the MDP and do not necessarily estimate $Q$ first.

Instead, directly parameterize and optimize the policy itself.

This lecture’s main new topic is this third family.

---

### 7. General form of value-based learning

For model-free Monte Carlo, SARSA, and Q-learning, the general update pattern is:

$$  
Q(s,a) \leftarrow Q(s,a) + \alpha \bigl(\text{target} - Q(s,a)\bigr)  
$$

where:

- $\alpha$ is the learning rate

- the **target** is some noisy estimate of what $Q(s,a)$ should be

This is just “move your estimate toward a target.”

---

### 8. On-policy vs off-policy

This is one of the most important distinctions in RL.

#### On-policy

The data is generated by the same policy whose value you are trying to estimate.

SARSA is on-policy.

It estimates:

$$  
Q_\pi  
$$

for the policy actually being followed.

#### Off-policy

The data can be generated by one policy, while you estimate another.

Q-learning is off-policy.

It estimates:

$$  
Q^*  
$$

even if the behavior policy is exploratory or random.

That is why Q-learning is so powerful conceptually: you can learn about the optimal policy from exploratory behavior.

---

### 9. Full rollouts vs bootstrapping

Another major design axis.

#### Full rollout target

Wait until the episode ends and use the actual utility observed.

This is Monte Carlo style.

#### Bootstrapped target

Use:

- immediate reward

- plus an estimate of future value from your current model

Example:

$$  
r + \gamma Q(s',a')  
$$

or

$$  
r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s',a')  
$$

This is bootstrapping.

You do not wait for the episode to finish; you lean on your current estimate of the future.

---

### 10. The tabular setting

So far, RL has been presented in a **tabular** way:

- every state–action pair has its own separate entry

- you can literally think of a lookup table or dictionary

This is fine for tiny MDPs.

It breaks in real problems:

- images as states

- long sentences as states

- complex robot configurations

- language-model generation settings

There are too many states to store one exact table entry for each one.

---

### 11. Function approximation

Function approximation replaces the lookup table with a parameterized function:

$$  
Q_\theta(s,a)  
$$

Instead of storing one value per pair, you learn parameters $\theta$ of a function.

The ML perspective now enters directly.

You must choose:

- a hypothesis class

- a loss function

- an optimization procedure

Exactly the same three questions from supervised learning.

---

### 12. Policy as classifier

A direct policy can also be parameterized.

A deterministic policy is hard to optimize directly, because hard argmax decisions are not smooth.

So, as in classification, we use a **probabilistic classifier**:

$$  
\pi_\theta(a \mid s)  
$$

This gives a distribution over actions for each state.

Because probabilities are differentiable, gradient-based optimization becomes possible.

This is the core bridge from RL into policy gradients.

---

### 13. Imitation learning

If expert demonstrations were available, policy learning would be much easier.

A demonstration rollout might produce examples like:

- state $1 \to$ walk

- state $2 \to$ walk

- state $3 \to$ tram

Then we could just do supervised learning:

$$  
J(\theta) = \sum_t \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_{t-1})  
$$

That is imitation learning: learn to copy expert behavior.

But in ordinary RL, the environment does **not** give demonstrations. It only gives rewards.

So we must somehow turn rewards into learning signals for the policy.

---

### 14. Trajectories

The lecture denotes a trajectory by $\tau$.

A typical trajectory is written as:

$$  
\tau = (s_0, a_1, r_1, s_1, a_2, r_2, s_2, \dots)  
$$

The indexing is slightly unusual compared with some textbooks, but the idea is standard:

- start in $s_0$

- choose an action

- get reward and next state

- repeat

---

### 15. Expected utility objective for policy learning

If the policy is parameterized by $\theta$, then the most direct RL objective is:

$$  
V(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_\theta[\text{utility}(\tau)]  
$$

Expanded explicitly:

$$  
V(\theta) = \sum_\tau p_\theta(\tau),\text{utility}(\tau)  
$$

This is extremely important.

It says: optimize the policy parameters so that the expected utility of a sampled trajectory is as large as possible.

This is the cleanest statement of the RL objective in the lecture.

---

### 16. Probability of a trajectory

The probability of a trajectory factors into alternating policy and environment terms:

# $$  
p_\theta(\tau)

p(s_0),  
\pi_\theta(a_1 \mid s_0),  
T(s_1 \mid s_0,a_1),  
\pi_\theta(a_2 \mid s_1),  
T(s_2 \mid s_1,a_2)\cdots  
$$

So trajectory probability comes from two sources:

- the policy

- the environment transition dynamics

This decomposition matters for the gradient derivation.

---

### 17. REINFORCE

REINFORCE is the simplest policy-gradient algorithm discussed.

Its core idea:

- sample trajectories from the current policy

- if a trajectory gets high utility, increase the probability of the actions that produced it

- if a trajectory gets low or negative utility, decrease the probability of the actions that produced it

In words, it is like imitation learning on your **own** trajectories, weighted by how good they turned out to be.

That is the right intuition to hold onto.

---

### 18. Variance reduction

Policy gradient estimates are noisy.

So the lecture introduces the central problem:

> Find a low-bias, low-variance estimate of the policy gradient.

Three key tools appear:

- baselines

- returns-to-go

- bootstrapped critics

These are the beginnings of actor-critic methods.

## 2. RL recap: agent, environment, reward loop

The lecture begins by re-grounding RL as a very general AI framework.

The cycle is:

1. the environment presents a state/observation

2. the agent chooses an action

3. the environment transitions and gives a reward

4. the agent updates its internal parameters

5. repeat

The point is that the agent never sees the full MDP description directly.

It only sees local experience such as:

- current state

- chosen action

- reward

- next state

- whether the episode ended

That local feedback is all the RL algorithm gets.

---

## 3. Example interaction from the agent’s point of view

The lecture explicitly walks through what the agent sees.

Example rollout:

- in state $1$, agent chooses walk

- environment returns next state $2$, reward $-1$

- in state $2$, agent chooses walk

- environment returns next state $3$, reward $-1$

- in state $3$, agent chooses tram

- environment returns next state $6$, reward $-2$, episode ends

If $\gamma = 1$, the utility is:

$$  
-1 + (-1) + (-2) = -4  
$$

This example is important because it shows that the agent does **not** observe “the true value.”  
It only sees one noisy trajectory realization.

That is why RL is fundamentally an estimation problem.

It prepares you to see later algorithms as different ways of choosing:

- **what object to estimate**

Policy-based methods estimate $\pi$.

## 5. Unifying view of previous RL algorithms

The lecture tries to prevent you from memorizing algorithms as isolated recipes.

Instead, it decomposes them into orthogonal choices.

### Choice A: model-based or model-free

- model-based: estimate transitions/rewards first

- model-free: estimate values or policy directly

### Choice B: on-policy or off-policy

- on-policy: learn about the same policy that generated data

- off-policy: learn about a different target policy

### Choice C: full rollout or bootstrapping

- full rollout: use complete return

- bootstrapping: use immediate reward plus estimated future value

This is exactly the right way to organize your mental model of RL.

## 6. Monte Carlo, SARSA, and Q-learning in one table

The lecture implicitly sets up the following comparison.

### Model-free Monte Carlo

Estimate $Q_\pi(s,a)$ using the actual utility from full rollouts.

Target:

$$  
\text{target} = \text{utility}(\tau)  
$$

Properties:

- on-policy

- no bootstrapping

- high variance

- unbiased as a return estimator

### SARSA

Estimate $Q_\pi(s,a)$ using the next action actually chosen by the policy.

Target:

$$  
\text{target} = r + \gamma Q(s',a')  
$$

where $a' = \pi(s')$.

Properties:

- on-policy

- bootstrapped

### Q-learning

Estimate $Q^*(s,a)$ using the greedy next action.

Target:

$$  
\text{target} = r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s',a')  
$$

Properties:

- off-policy

- bootstrapped

The lecture stresses that the difference between SARSA and Q-learning is not in `get_action`; it is in the **target** inside the update.

That is a very common confusion, so keep it fixed in memory.

## 7. Why tabular methods break

The lecture now shifts toward large-scale RL.

Tabular methods assume you can store a separate quantity for each $(s,a)$ pair.

That fails when states are huge objects like:

- images

- paragraphs

- robot sensor configurations

- token histories in language modeling

You cannot index a table by “all possible images.”

So the key question becomes:

> How do we generalize knowledge across similar states?

That is exactly what function approximation is for.

---

## 8. Function approximation for Q-learning

The lecture introduces:

$$  
Q_\theta(s,a)  
$$

Instead of storing numbers directly, we compute them from parameters $\theta$.

The general recipe is:

1. map $(s,a)$ to features $\phi(s,a)$

2. feed those features into a model

3. output a scalar Q-value

Examples:

### Linear model

$$  
Q_\theta(s,a) = \theta^\top \phi(s,a)  
$$

### MLP / neural network

$$  
Q_\theta(s,a) = \text{MLP}_\theta(\phi(s,a))  
$$

This is standard supervised-learning architecture plugged into RL.

## 9. The three ML design questions reappear

The lecture explicitly reminds you of the ML framework:

### Hypothesis class

What family of functions are allowed?  
Linear? MLP? Something else?

### Loss function

How do we know whether current predictions are good?

### Optimization algorithm

How do we update parameters to reduce loss?

This is one of the main conceptual bridges of the lecture:

> Modern RL = RL objectives + ML function approximation.

## 10. Simulating tabular Q-learning with one-hot features

To keep the example simple, the lecture uses one-hot features for each $(s,a)$ pair.

That means each state–action pair maps to a unique basis vector.

Conceptually:

- $(1,\text{walk})$ gets one one-hot vector

- $(1,\text{tram})$ gets another

- $(2,\text{walk})$ gets another

- etc.

This does **not** really exploit generalization.  
It is just a way to show that a parameterized model can reproduce the tabular setup.

So the point is pedagogical, not to claim one-hot features are powerful.

---

## 11. Policy extraction from parameterized Q-values

Once you can compute Q-values, the policy is still derived by greedification:

$$  
\pi(s) = \arg\max_a Q_\theta(s,a)  
$$

So even with function approximation, value-based RL remains **indirect policy learning**:

1. learn values

2. derive policy from values

That motivates the next question:

> If the policy is what we ultimately care about, why not parameterize that directly?

---

## 12. Parameterized Q-learning loss

The update uses a bootstrapped target just like ordinary Q-learning:

$$  
\text{target} =  
\begin{cases}  
r & \text{if terminal}\  
r + \gamma Q_\theta(s', \pi(s')) & \text{otherwise}  
\end{cases}  
$$

In the lecture’s implementation, the loss is ordinary squared loss:

$$  
L(\theta) = \bigl(Q_\theta(s,a) - \text{target}\bigr)^2  
$$

Then SGD updates $\theta$.

So the overall logic is:

- RL tells you what the target should be

- ML tells you how to fit predictions to that target

That division of labor is a very useful way to think.

---

## 13. Important caveat: function approximation changes the theory

In tabular settings, Q-learning has classical convergence guarantees under suitable conditions.

With general function approximation, those guarantees largely disappear.

The lecture explicitly points out that with function approximation:

- convergence to the true optimal policy is not guaranteed in general

- you may only hope for local optima or approximate solutions

- initialization and exploration matter much more

This is one reason deep RL is powerful but also fragile.

## 14. Transition to policy-based methods

Now the lecture makes the big conceptual move.

We had:

- model-based: estimate MDP, then solve for policy

- value-based: estimate $Q$, then derive policy

The next question is:

> Do we need either of those intermediate objects?

Could we just learn the policy directly?

The lecture answers: yes, and that leads to policy gradient.

---

## 15. Policy as probabilistic classifier

A deterministic policy is not easy to optimize with gradients.

So we define a stochastic policy:

$$  
\pi_\theta(a \mid s)  
$$

This is exactly analogous to probabilistic classification in supervised learning.

Input:

$$  
s  
$$

Output:

$$  
\text{distribution over actions}  
$$

In practice, the model outputs logits and then applies softmax:

$$  
\pi_\theta(a \mid s) = \text{softmax}(\text{logits}_\theta(s))  
$$

This gives two advantages:

- differentiability

- built-in exploration through sampling

---

## 16. Imitation learning warm-up

Before doing policy gradient, the lecture gives the easier case.

If an expert demonstrates the right actions, then policy learning is just supervised learning.

Objective:

$$  
J(\theta) = \sum_t \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_{t-1})  
$$

Maximizing this says:

> increase probability of the expert action in the expert state.

This is ordinary behavioral cloning / imitation learning.

The examples given are:

- robotics teleoperation

- human-written math solutions

The key contrast is that in RL we do **not** get expert-labeled actions, only rewards.

---

## 17. The core RL challenge for policy learning

In RL, we only know how good an entire trajectory was.

We do **not** get told:

- which action was correct at each step

- which exact state-action pair deserves praise or blame

So the policy-gradient problem is:

> how can we turn a trajectory-level reward signal into parameter updates for individual action probabilities?

The lecture answers this by differentiating expected utility over trajectories.

---

## 18. Probability of a trajectory

A trajectory’s probability under policy $\pi_\theta$ is:

# $$  
p_\theta(\tau)

p(s_0)\prod_t \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_{t-1}),T(s_t \mid s_{t-1},a_t)  
$$

This matters because the expected utility objective is:

$$  
V(\theta) = \sum_\tau p_\theta(\tau),\text{utility}(\tau)  
$$

The policy affects utility **indirectly** by changing which trajectories become likely.

That is the key conceptual move.

We are not differentiating reward directly.  
We are differentiating the probability of trajectories that lead to reward.

---

## 19. Deriving the policy gradient

Start with:

$$  
V(\theta) = \sum_\tau p_\theta(\tau),\text{utility}(\tau)  
$$

Take the gradient:

# $$  
\nabla_\theta V(\theta)

\sum_\tau \nabla_\theta p_\theta(\tau),\text{utility}(\tau)  
$$

Now apply the log-derivative trick:

# $$  
\nabla_\theta p_\theta(\tau)

p_\theta(\tau)\nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(\tau)  
$$

So:

# $$  
\nabla_\theta V(\theta)

\sum_\tau p_\theta(\tau)\nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(\tau),\text{utility}(\tau)  
$$

Therefore:

# $$  
\nabla_\theta V(\theta)

\mathbb{E}_\theta\bigl[\nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(\tau),\text{utility}(\tau)\bigr]  
$$

This is the policy-gradient identity presented in the lecture.

---

## 20. Why this identity is useful

The expectation above is over all trajectories, which is intractable in large problems.

But expectations can be approximated by samples.

So if you sample one trajectory $\tau \sim p_\theta(\tau)$, an unbiased estimator of the gradient is:

# $$  
\nabla_\theta J(\theta,\tau)

\nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(\tau),\text{utility}(\tau)  
$$

This is the critical operational step:

- interact with environment

- get one trajectory

- use it to estimate the gradient

- update the policy

This is what turns the theory into an algorithm.

---

## 21. Why only policy terms matter in the gradient

This is an important detail that is easy to miss.

Expand the log trajectory probability:

# $$  
\log p_\theta(\tau)

\log p(s_0)  
+  
\sum_t \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_{t-1})  
+  
\sum_t \log T(s_t \mid s_{t-1},a_t)  
$$

Now differentiate with respect to $\theta$.

The environment terms do not depend on policy parameters, so they vanish.

Thus:

# $$  
\nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(\tau)

\sum_t \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_{t-1})  
$$

Substitute into the gradient estimator:

# $$  
\nabla_\theta J(\theta,\tau)

\text{utility}(\tau)\sum_t \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_{t-1})  
$$

This is the REINFORCE form.

That is the exact mathematical reason policy gradient can be computed without knowing the environment transition model.

---

## 22. Intuition for REINFORCE

REINFORCE says:

- sample a trajectory from your current policy

- if the total utility is high, increase the log-probability of the actions you took

- if the total utility is low or negative, reduce the log-probability of those actions

So it is not using labeled correct actions from an expert.

It is using the trajectory’s overall outcome as the weight.

The lecture gives a very good intuition:

> It is like imitation learning on your own trajectories, weighted by utility.

A particularly clean special case:

- if utility is $1$ for success and $0$ for failure,

- then REINFORCE becomes imitation learning on your own successful rollouts.

That is a very useful intuition.

---

## 23. REINFORCE is on-policy

This matters.

The gradient estimate is taken under the current policy distribution:

$$  
\tau \sim p_\theta(\tau)  
$$

So the data must come from the current policy.

That means REINFORCE is inherently **on-policy**.

You cannot freely reuse arbitrary off-policy data in the basic derivation.

---

## 24. REINFORCE implementation in the lecture

The lecture implements a stochastic policy with a linear model from one-hot state features to logits over actions.

### Step 1: map state to features

For the toy example, state $s$ becomes a one-hot vector.

### Step 2: compute logits

The model outputs a vector of raw scores, one per action.

### Step 3: apply softmax

This converts logits into probabilities:

$$  
\pi_\theta(a \mid s)  
$$

### Step 4: sample an action

The action is sampled from this distribution.

That means exploration is built into the policy itself.

No separate epsilon-greedy exploration policy is strictly required.

---

## 25. Episode-level update in REINFORCE

The lecture stores the entire rollout and total utility.

Only when the episode ends does it update the parameters.

That makes REINFORCE resemble Monte Carlo methods:

- it uses full-rollout information

- it waits until the return is known

- it does not bootstrap from estimated future values

So REINFORCE is best thought of as a **Monte Carlo policy-gradient method**.

That is why it is unbiased but noisy.

---

## 26. Cross-entropy interpretation

Conceptually, the update wants to maximize:

$$  
\sum_t \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_{t-1})  
$$

weighted by trajectory utility.

In modern ML libraries, this often appears as cross-entropy loss.

Why?

Because minimizing cross-entropy for target action $a_t$ is equivalent to maximizing $\log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s)$.

So the policy-gradient update can be viewed as:

- classification loss on chosen actions

- weighted by how good the trajectory turned out to be

This is the cleanest way to connect policy gradient to supervised learning.

---

## 27. Important implementation caveat from the lecture

The lecturer explicitly notes a bug/correction in the implementation discussion:

the loss should be weighted by utility.

Conceptually, the update should reflect:

# $$  
L(\theta)

-\text{utility}(\tau)\sum_t \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_{t-1})  
$$

or equivalently an implementation using cross-entropy scaled by the return.

The sign convention depends on whether you are doing gradient ascent on reward or gradient descent on loss, but the conceptual rule is fixed:

- good trajectories should increase action probabilities

- bad trajectories should decrease them

Do not lose that conceptual anchor in the implementation details.

---

## 28. Initialization matters more in policy gradient

The lecture points out something subtle but important.

In value-based methods, poor initial values are bad but not necessarily fatal.

In policy-gradient methods, initial policy parameters directly affect:

- what actions are sampled

- what data is collected

- therefore what learning signal is even available

So a bad initialization can create poor exploration.

That is why small initial parameters, which produce near-uniform softmax probabilities, are often preferable.

You want broad initial exploration.

---

## 29. Why REINFORCE has high variance

Even though REINFORCE is unbiased, its estimates can vary a lot from episode to episode.

Why?

Because one sampled trajectory may be unusually lucky or unlucky.

Its utility is therefore a noisy estimate of how good the policy really is.

This creates unstable gradients and slower learning.

So the next part of the lecture is about improving the gradient estimator.

---

## 30. Variance reduction via a toy mean-estimation example

The lecture uses a simple statistics detour.

Suppose you want to estimate a mean:

$$  
\mu = \mathbb{E}[f(i)] = \sum_i p(i)f(i)  
$$

Different estimators can have:

- the same mean

- different variances

Examples:

- sample one point: unbiased but noisy

- sample two points and average: still unbiased, lower variance

- add random noise: still unbiased, higher variance

The lesson is:

> “Unbiased” is not enough. Variance matters.

This is directly analogous to policy gradient estimation.

---

## 31. Control variates / baselines

Now comes the important trick.

If you can subtract something with expectation zero, you do not change the mean of the estimator, but you may reduce its variance.

In RL, the analogous identity is:

If $b(s)$ depends only on the state and not on the chosen action, then

$$  
\mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid s)}  
\bigl[  
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a \mid s), b(s)  
\bigr] = 0  
$$

This is the baseline identity from the lecture.

So you can replace utility by utility minus baseline:

$$  
\text{utility}(\tau) ;\to; \text{utility}(\tau) - b(s)  
$$

without changing the expected gradient.

That preserves unbiasedness but can reduce variance dramatically.

---

## 32. Why the baseline must not depend on action

This restriction is essential.

The zero-expectation proof works because $b(s)$ can be pulled outside the sum over actions.

If the baseline depended on $a$, that cancellation would generally fail.

So a baseline can depend on:

- the state

- time step

- perhaps learned value predictions tied to the state

But in the simple theorem, it should not depend on the sampled action itself.

---

## 33. Returns-to-go

The lecture then refines the weighting signal.

Instead of using the full trajectory utility for every action in the trajectory, use only future rewards from that point onward.

Define the return-to-go from time $t$:

$$  
G_t = r_t + \gamma r_{t+1} + \gamma^2 r_{t+2} + \cdots  
$$

Then use:

$$  
\sum_t \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_{t-1}),(G_t - b(s_{t-1}))  
$$

Why is this better?

Because rewards that happened before action $a_t$ cannot have been caused by $a_t$.

So including past rewards only adds noise.

Returns-to-go reduce that unnecessary variance.

---

## 34. Bootstrapping re-enters: actor-critic intuition

The lecture’s final enhancement brings back value estimation.

Instead of weighting policy updates by full return, use an estimate such as $Q(s,a)$ or a related value estimate.

This gives methods of the form:

$$  
\sum_t \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t),(Q(s_t,a_t)-b(s_t))  
$$

This introduces some bias if the value estimate is imperfect, but often reduces variance enough to make learning much more practical.

This is the entry point to **actor-critic** methods:

- **actor** = the policy

- **critic** = the value estimator used to guide the policy update

A common modern interpretation is to use the baseline as a value function:

$$  
b(s_t) = V(s_t)  
$$

Then the quantity

$$  
Q(s_t,a_t) - V(s_t)  
$$

is the **advantage**.

That says how much better the chosen action was than the state’s typical value.

Even if the lecture does not push this notation fully, that is the natural conceptual endpoint.

## 35. Grand summary of the lecture’s RL landscape

By the end, the lecture organizes RL as:

### Model-based

Estimate MDP, then solve for policy.

### Value-based

Estimate values, then derive policy.

### Policy-based

Directly parameterize and optimize the policy.

And all three share a similar outer loop:

1. generate experience

2. form a learning objective or target

3. take a gradient/update step

That is the high-level unification the lecture is aiming for.

## Summary

Lecture 9 is fundamentally about a conceptual progression:

$$  
\text{MDP} \to \text{Values} \to \text{Policy}  
$$

The lecture first reviews RL and shows how previous algorithms differ along a few important axes:

- model-based vs model-free

- on-policy vs off-policy

- full-rollout targets vs bootstrapping

- tabular vs function approximation

It then shows how to scale value-based RL using function approximation:

$$  
Q_\theta(s,a)  
$$

This imports the machinery of supervised learning into RL:

- choose features/model

- define a loss

- optimise with gradient methods

Then the lecture asks the deeper question:

> Why estimate values at all if the policy is what we ultimately care about?

That leads to policy gradient.

The direct objective is:

$$  
V(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_\theta[\text{utility}(\tau)]  
$$

Differentiating this and applying the log-derivative trick gives:

# $$  
\nabla_\theta V(\theta)

\mathbb{E}_\theta  
\bigl[  
\nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(\tau),\text{utility}(\tau)  
\bigr]  
$$

Since only the policy terms depend on $\theta$:

# $$  
\nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(\tau)

\sum_t \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_{t-1})  
$$

which yields the REINFORCE update:

# $$  
\nabla_\theta J(\theta,\tau)

\text{utility}(\tau)  
\sum_t \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_{t-1})  
$$

Intuitively, REINFORCE is imitation learning on your own sampled behavior, weighted by how good the resulting trajectory was.

Its strengths:

- direct policy optimization

- conceptually clean

- unbiased gradient estimator

Its weakness:

- very high variance

So the lecture introduces three improvements:

- subtract a **baseline** $b(s)$ to reduce variance without changing the expected gradient

- use **returns-to-go** instead of total episode utility

- use a learned value estimate, leading toward **actor-critic** methods

If you leave this lecture with one central idea, it should be this:

> Policy gradient works by increasing the probability of action sequences that lead to better returns, using sampled trajectories as stochastic gradient estimates of expected utility.

## Real-World Applications

### Robotics

A robot arm may observe an image and output motor actions.

Tabular methods are impossible because states are high-dimensional.  
Function approximation and policy optimization are essential.

Imitation learning is often used first from teleoperated demonstrations, then RL fine-tunes behavior.

---

### Game playing and control

In many control tasks, the policy itself is the natural object to optimize.

Stochastic policies are useful for exploration, especially when deterministic greedy behavior gets stuck.

---

### Language models and RLHF-style settings

A state may be:

- a prompt

- a dialogue history

- a partially generated response

An action may be the next token or a higher-level response choice.

Policy-based ideas are central because the model already naturally defines a distribution over actions.

---

### Math and reasoning tasks

Human-written solutions can provide imitation-learning data.

Reward signals can then be layered on top to push beyond simple supervised imitation.

---

### Large-state environments generally

Any setting with:

- images

- text

- complex sensor streams

- continuous inputs

pushes you away from tabular RL and toward function approximation, direct policy learning, or actor-critic methods.
